Machu Picchu




This beloved UNESCO World Heritage site is by far the ultimate travel experience and highest point of any getaway in Peru or arguably in the whole of the South American region. It’s one of the world’s most visited ancient wonders of the World, you simply have to experience it for yourself to be mesmerized by the grandeur and mystique of Machu Picchu.



Machu Picchu – South America’s finest and most spectacular treasure – is a magnificent ancient city that lures, mystifies and intrigues travelers and historians with its iconic and awe-inspiring Incan Ruins. Nicknamed fittingly as the “Lost City of the Incas”

At any given time, the Plitvice Lakes never look the same as they did the day before. The lakes are consequently notorious for their fluctuating water colours. The water colours range from a bright emerald green, a deep blue, or a faded grey. The lakes are continuously changing colour according to the amount of minerals or organisms, as well as the angle of sunlight. The most important ingredient causing the water to turn blue or green is calcium carbonate from limestone rocks.

Filipinos who intend to travel to Peru for leisure and tourism are not required to secure a visa prior to their arrival. As a matter of fact, you can stay up to 183 days in the country, as long as you have a Philippine Passport that’s valid for at least 6 months beyond your trip to Peru. Also, Pinoys who are traveling only to Machu Picchu and Cuzco won’t need vaccinations against malaria and yellow fever.


How to Get There?

Machu Picchu is nestled on a mountain ledge that’s over a hundred meters above the river and valley. There’s no direct flight route to this ancient city from Manila, and you are going to use a combination of transportation options to reach this magnificent site.

Step 1: Fly to Lima
To get there, first you need to fly from Manila International Airport to Jorge Chavez International Airport in Lima – the largest city and the capital of Peru. Travel time ranges from 28 to 40 hours, including a couple of stops or more to cities like Los Angles, Tokyo, Hong Kong and Mexico City. Just use our mobile flights app or our desktop search tool, to find the best and cheapest flights from the Philippines to Lima.

Step 2: Travel to Cusco
From Lima, travel to Cusco via air or bus. Of course, the bus ride is the cheaper option, but it’s going to take over 20 hours of travel. If you’re tight on travel time, it will save you lots of time if you fly instead and if you book your tickets early, you could get a good deal!

Step 3: Allow your body to adjust to the altitude
Cusco, with an elevation of 3,399 meters (11,152 feet) above sea level, can cause altitude sickness even to the most experienced Pinoy outdoor enthusiasts. And, make no mistake about it – altitude can do quite a ton of strange and unwanted things to your body, such as dizziness, vomiting, diarrhea and difficulty breathing. Luckily, there are proven ways to help your body get acclimated to Cusco’s altitude.

Step 4: Travel to Machu Picchu
There are two ways to get to Machu Picchu from Cusco – the Inca Trail and public transportation. The Inca Trail is a tough and challenging 4-day, 3-night trek ideal for intrepid, fit and experienced outdoor lovers. Do take note that no one is allowed to trek independently along the Inca Trail, and everyone is required to travel with a sanctioned group or a tour agency.



Other Places to Visit in Peru:

The Inca Trail

The famous Inca Trail is a four-day hike, which terminates at Machu Picchu, and is regarded by many as the highlight of their trip to Peru. This scenic trail is often more demanding than what many people are expecting, but also more rewarding.



Cusco's Architectural Treasures

Walking through the streets of Cusco is like wandering through a museum, with history built upon history in this UNESCO World Heritage Site. Inca ruins have been used in the foundations of many of the lovely old colonial buildings lining the narrow roads, showcasing the city's long history.


Isla Amantani

The sparkling blue water of Lake Titicaca is surrounded by rolling hills and traditional small villages. The lake area is a mix of beautiful scenery and culture that sets it apart from other regions of the country. Sitting at 3,820 meters above sea level, Lake Titicaca is known for being the highest navigable lake in the world.


Colca Canyon (Cañon del Colca)

The canyon reaches a depth of 3,400 meters and is the result of a seismic fault between two volcanoes. At the base far below is a winding river.Colca Canyon area has been inhabited for thousands of years and was home to the Collagua, Cabana, and eventually the Inca peoples. Stone terracing along the canyon walls dates to AD 800 and is still in use today.

Amazon River at Puerto Maldonado

Just a half hour flight from Cusco, Puerto Maldonado is a key jumping-off point for tours of the Amazon. This is a completely different experience than what you will find in other parts of Peru, with hot humid jungle and a chance to see all kinds of unique wildlife. Caimans, capybara, monkeys, parrots, turtles, and piranhas are what you can expect to find in this part of the country.

Ica and the Sand Dunes at Huacachina

For the sporting type looking to try something a little different, the oasis resort of Huacachina on the outskirts of Ica has just the answer. This picture-perfect, palm-fringed resort town just west of Ica is situated around a lagoon surrounded by huge sand dunes, some of which reach 1,000 meters in height.People come here to try out the sport of sandboarding.
